Car Loans During Bankruptcy

A bankruptcy car loan situation can go several ways depending on your chapter and whether you want to keep or surrender the vehicle:

  • Keep and reaffirm: Sign a reaffirmation agreement to keep the car and continue payments on original terms
  • Keep and cramdown (Ch. 13): Reduce the loan balance to the car's current value if you have owned it 910+ days
  • Surrender: Return the car, discharge the deficiency balance, and start fresh
  • Redeem (Ch. 7): Pay the car's current value in one lump sum to keep it free and clear

Chapter 13 Car Cramdown

One of Chapter 13's most powerful tools is the car cramdown under 11 U.S.C. Section 506(a). If you purchased your car more than 910 days before filing, you can reduce the loan balance to the car's current fair market value. This can save thousands - a car worth $12,000 with a $20,000 loan balance becomes a $12,000 claim.

The interest rate on the crammed-down loan is also typically reduced to the Till rate (prime + 1-3%, currently around 6-8%).

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my car if I file bankruptcy?

Yes, in most cases. You can reaffirm the loan, cramdown the loan in Chapter 13, or redeem the car in Chapter 7. Your vehicle exemption protects equity up to your state's limit.

What is a bankruptcy car loan cramdown?

A cramdown reduces your car loan balance to the vehicle's current fair market value in Chapter 13. If you owe $20,000 on a car worth $12,000, the secured claim becomes $12,000. The car must have been purchased 910+ days before filing.

Can I get a car loan during Chapter 13 bankruptcy?

Yes, with court approval. File a motion to incur new debt showing the purchase is necessary and affordable. Courts generally approve necessary vehicle purchases.

Foundational Caselaw

The cases below define the doctrine on this site:

  • Associates Commercial Corp. v. Rash, 520 U.S. 953 (1997) - Section 506(a) secured-claim valuation in cramdown uses the "replacement value" standard (what the debtor would have to pay to replace the property in its current condition), not the foreclosure-sale value.
  • Till v. SCS Credit Corp., 541 U.S. 465 (2004) - cramdown interest rate in Chapter 13 is the prime rate plus a risk adjustment (1-3%); formula approach controls.
  • Dewsnup v. Timm, 502 U.S. 410 (1992) - Section 506(d) does NOT permit Chapter 7 debtors to "strip down" a partially secured lien to the collateral's value.
  • Bank of America, N.A. v. Caulkett, 575 U.S. 790 (2015) - extends Dewsnup; even wholly-underwater junior liens cannot be voided in Chapter 7.
